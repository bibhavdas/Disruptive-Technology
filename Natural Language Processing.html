Natural Language Processing

Imagine trying to speak to a computer in plain English, and instead of giving you an error code, it actually understands you and replies thoughtfully. That is the magic of Natural Language Processing (NLP). It bridges the critical gap between human communication and computational understanding.

A subfield of linguistics, computer science, and artificial intelligence concerned with the interactions between computers and human language, specifically how to program computers to process and analyze large amounts of natural language data.
In simple words: NLP enables computers to understand, interpret, analyze, and generate human languages in a meaningful way.

Real-World Applications

  • Machine Translation: Converting text from one language to another (e.g., Google Translate, ElevenLabs).
  • Virtual Assistants: Voice-activated helpers bridging spoken word and digital action.
  • Chatbots: Automated agents for customer service and inquiry.
  • Spam Detection: Scanning semantics to filter malicious or junk correspondence.
  • Sentiment Analysis: Determining if a writer's mood is positive, negative, or neutral.

Unstructured vs. Structured Data

Human language is inherently messy and chaotic—this is known as Unstructured Data. Consider the sentence: "Bring A4 sheet & Marker to class." To a computer, this is merely a random string of characters.

NLP's fundamental job is to extract the meaning from that chaos and turn it into Structured Data. It identifies the entities and intent, effectively creating a neatly organized list containing "A4 sheet" and "Marker" so the system knows exactly what items are required.

The Two Branches of NLP

Just as human conversation requires both listening and speaking, NLP is split into two foundational branches:

  • NLU (Natural Language Understanding): The computer's ability to "listen", comprehend, and derive meaning from what we are saying or typing.
  • NLG (Natural Language Generation): The computer's ability to "speak", write, or formulate responses back to us in standard, coherent human language.

Text Processing Techniques

To make a computer comprehend a full paragraph, we must first break the text down into bite-sized, mathematical pieces. Computer scientists utilize several vital processing techniques to achieve this.

A. Tokenization

The process of demarcating and possibly classifying sections of a string of input characters into individual, atomic components called tokens.
In simple words: Splitting raw text into smaller, meaningful units so the computer can process and analyze the language piece by piece.

For example, the sentence "Bring A4 sheets & Markers to class." is chopped up into individual tokens: "Bring", "A4", "sheets", "&", "Markers", "to", "class", ".".

B. Stemming

A heuristic process that chops off the ends of words in the hope of achieving the goal of reducing inflectional forms to a common base form.
In simple words: A quick-and-dirty technique that reduces a word to its root or base form by simply removing the suffix (end) or prefix (beginning) of the word.

Example: The word going gets mechanically chopped down to its root word: go.

C. Lemmatization

The algorithmic process of determining the lemma of a word based on its intended meaning, involving morphological analysis and vocabulary.
In simple words: A smarter, more advanced version of stemming. Instead of blindly chopping letters, it considers grammar and meaningful context to reduce a word to its true, base dictionary form.

This base dictionary form is called a LEMMA. It ensures the resulting word is an actual, valid word you could look up in a standard dictionary.

D. POS Tagging (Parts of Speech Tagging)

The process of marking up a word in a text as corresponding to a particular part of speech, based on both its definition and its context.
In simple words: A tagging technique that assigns a grammatical label to each word in a sentence based on its role (e.g., identifying nouns, verbs, adjectives).

E. NER (Named/Natural Entity Recognition)

Information extraction that seeks to locate and classify named entities mentioned in unstructured text into pre-defined categories.
In simple words: A technique that scans text, identifies real-world entities, and classifies them into predefined categories like persons, locations, and organizations.

*Note: While widely known as "Named Entity Recognition" in the broader tech industry, the core function remains the identification of natural, real-world entities.
